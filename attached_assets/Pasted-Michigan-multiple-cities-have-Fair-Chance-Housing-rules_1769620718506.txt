Michigan: multiple cities have Fair Chance Housing rules (criminal history limits)
Ann Arbor, MI

Effect: prohibits using criminal history in tenant selection (with limited exceptions).

Detroit, MI

Effect: delays criminal history questions until after a conditional lease + prohibits using arrest records (among other restrictions).

Jackson, MI

Effect: bans asking about criminal history during initial screening and postpones background check until a conditional lease is offered.

Flint, MI

Effect: adopted a Fair Chance Housing ordinance (criminal history protections).

(There are other Michigan cities mentioned in reporting, but the above are the ones you should hard-code first because they’re well documented and meaningfully change workflow.)

LeaseShield logic implication: In these cities, “criminal history” should often be Not Allowed or Extra Steps Required, and the UI should guide “conditional offer / individualized assessment” where required.

2) California: major city-level Fair Chance rules (criminal history)
Oakland, CA – Fair Chance Housing Ordinance

Effect: generally prohibits screening criminal history during advertisement/application/selection (very strong restriction).

Berkeley, CA – Fair Chance / “ban-the-box” style housing rules

Effect: city guidance/FAQ documents for the ordinance; practical restrictions on criminal history screening.

San Francisco, CA – Fair Chance (notably focused on affordable housing providers)

Effect: fair chance protections tied to affordable housing providers.

LeaseShield logic implication: California isn’t just “state rules.” You’ll want city overrides for places like Oakland/Berkeley/SF because the compliance posture changes dramatically.

3) Illinois: Cook County “Just Housing Amendment” (county-level criminal history rules)
Cook County, IL (suburban Cook; Chicago has its own separate rules)

Effect: prohibits denial based on arrest records and requires an individualized assessment for covered criminal history before denying.

LeaseShield logic implication: If “Cook County (suburban)” is selected, criminal history becomes Extra Steps Required (individualized assessment), and “arrest-only” should be Not Allowed.

4) Arizona: multiple cities have Source of Income rules (vouchers)

Arizona is sneaky because state law does not blanket-protect vouchers the way some states do, but several major cities do.

Phoenix, AZ – Source of Income protection

Effect: Phoenix explicitly lists “source of income” as protected for housing discrimination.

Tucson, AZ – Source of Income ordinance

Effect: Tucson has a dedicated SOI ordinance site and explains it’s unlawful to reject solely due to source of income (including vouchers).

Tempe, AZ – Source of Income protection

Effect: Tempe code updates include SOI protection including Housing Choice Voucher.

LeaseShield logic implication: In these cities, “Uses housing voucher” should be Not Allowed as a denial reason (unless you build a very narrow exception flow).

5) Virginia: state-level source-of-income protection (not just city)

Virginia is simpler than Arizona here:

Effect: “Source of funds/income” became protected under Virginia fair housing law (statewide).

LeaseShield logic implication: For VA, voucher-based denial should generally be Not Allowed statewide.

How I’d implement this in LeaseShield (simple, scalable)
A) Add a “High-Risk Jurisdiction Overrides” layer (first)

Hard-code the jurisdictions above as “special handling,” because they’re the ones that cause the biggest landlord mistakes.

B) Classify each override into one of 3 rule types

Criminal history prohibited (Ann Arbor-style)

Criminal history delayed / individualized assessment required (Detroit/Cook County-style)

Source of income protected (Phoenix/Tucson/Tempe/VA-style)

C) In the UI, show a one-line banner when triggered

Example:

Local Rule: This city restricts use of criminal history in rental decisions.

No legalese. Just clarity.
